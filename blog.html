Technology & Everyday Vulnerabilities

Today’s technologies have transformed almost every aspect of modern life. Smartphones, laptops, cloud services, and artificial intelligence make communication faster, learning easier, and work more efficient. However, these benefits also come with serious vulnerabilities. Cyberattacks, data breaches, identity theft, and privacy invasion have become common concerns in a world where so much personal information is being stored online. Even simple activities such as using social media, online banking, or downloading applications can expose users to risks if security measures are weak.

In my opinion, technological vulnerabilities should never be ignored. Many people assume that cybercrime only affects large companies or famous individuals, but ordinary users are also targets. Hackers can steal passwords, financial information, or private data through phishing emails, unsafe websites, or insecure public Wi-Fi networks. In addition, devices themselves can track user behavior and collect large amounts of personal information, sometimes without people fully realizing it.

When interacting with devices like mobile phones and laptops, I believe it is very important to acknowledge these risks and take precautions. I would avoid ignoring security warnings and instead practice safe digital habits. For example, using strong passwords, enabling two-factor authentication, updating software regularly, being careful about what links or files are opened and clearing your browser cache on a regular basis can greatly reduce risks.

Overall, today’s technologies are extremely valuable, but users must remain aware of their vulnerabilities. Responsible and informed use of technology is the best way to enjoy its advantages while minimizing potential dangers.

SWOT Analysis

Benefits & Risks of Evolving Technology

Technology is constantly evolving, bringing both major opportunities and significant challenges to society. From artificial intelligence to advanced communication systems, innovation improves the way people work, study, travel, and interact. However, rapid technological progress also creates risks that individuals and organizations must carefully manage.

A useful way to evaluate technology is through a SWOT analysis. The strengths of evolving technology include increased productivity, faster communication, medical advancements, and easier access to information. Weaknesses involve overdependence on devices, reduced face-to-face interaction, and growing cybersecurity concerns. Opportunities include the creation of new industries, smarter education systems, environmental solutions, and global collaboration. Threats involve job displacement due to automation, privacy violations, cybercrime, and the spread of misinformation.

One important aspect to focus on is cybersecurity, which fits under both weaknesses and threats. As technology becomes more advanced, cyberattacks also become more sophisticated. Personal data stored on smartphones, laptops, and cloud platforms can be stolen or misused if systems are not properly protected. Businesses, hospitals, and even governments have become targets of hackers, showing how vulnerable modern society can be. At the same time, people often share personal information online without fully understanding the risks involved.

Despite these dangers, technology should not be viewed negatively. Instead, society should focus on responsible innovation and stronger security practices. Developers can improve systems with better encryption, safer software, and regular penetration testing to identify weaknesses before hackers can exploit them. Penetration testing allows companies and organizations to simulate cyberattacks and strengthen their security systems in advance. At the same time, users can protect themselves through strong passwords, regular software updates, and careful online behavior. In my opinion, the benefits of technological progress outweigh the risks, but only if individuals and organizations remain aware of the challenges and actively work to reduce them.

Is AI Making Us Dumber?

Matthias Krüger

Where should we draw the line between using AI to support learning and relying on it to think for us?

Artificial intelligence should be used as a support for learning, not a replacement for thinking. There is a fine line between using AI to guide us and letting it do all the work for us. When we use AI to explain a difficult idea, suggest an outline, or give feedback on our writing, it can help us understand things better and make learning easier. In this way, AI acts like a tutor or tool that helps us learn more effectively.

However, if we rely on AI to complete all our assignments without ever actually engaging with the material on our own, we miss the purpose of education. The goal of learning is to help us develop as a person and gain a variety of skills, such as problem-solving, creativity or to think for ourselves. If AI always thinks for us, those skills are bound to weaken over time.

For that reason, AI should be used carefully and responsibly. It should help us learn, improve, and reflect on our work, but it should never replace our own effort.

Tim Biedermann

Where should we draw the line between using AI to support learning and relying on it to think for us?

AI can be helpful when it is used to improve our own work. For example, it is perfectly acceptable to use AI to check grammar, correct spelling mistakes, or suggest better ways to phrase sentences. These functions help students communicate their ideas more clearly without replacing their own thinking. However, problems arise when AI is used to generate entire texts, essays, or assignments. In such cases, students are no longer developing creativity or writing skills themselves.

Learning is not only about producing a correct answer. It is also about understanding the process behind it and learning from mistakes. When students write their own texts, they practice organizing ideas, building arguments, and expressing their opinions. If AI does all the thinking and writing, students miss valuable opportunities to learn and improve. As a result, they may become dependent on technology instead of developing their own abilities.

The line should be drawn where AI stops assisting and starts doing the intellectual work on our behalf.

Digital Identity

Matthias Krüger

If AI can identify us continuously, will privacy still exist?

Artificial intelligence is making continuous identification increasingly common. Today, AI systems can recognize faces, voices, locations, and even patterns of behavior with impressive accuracy. As these tools become more widely used, they are also more likely to reshape the meaning of privacy in everyday life.

There are clear benefits to this technology. Continuous identification can improve security, simplify access to digital services, and help organizations confirm identities more efficiently. It can also reduce fraud and support public safety in situations where quick and accurate identification is essential.

At the same time, constant identification raises serious privacy concerns. If people are continuously tracked and monitored, they may lose control over their personal information. Their movements, purchases, and online activity could potentially be recorded, stored, and analyzed without their full awareness. Over time, this may create a society in which individuals feel uncomfortable expressing themselves freely because of the constant fear of always being observed.

For privacy to continue to exist, clear laws, ethical guidelines and a responsible use of this technology will be necessary. AI should be designed to respect personal boundaries and individual rights rather than eliminate them.

Tim Biedermann

Should people have the right to refuse biometric authentication?

People should have the right to refuse biometric authentication because it involves highly sensitive personal data. Biometric identifiers such as fingerprints and facial features are unique and cannot be changed if compromised, unlike passwords. This makes their collection and storage a significant privacy and security risk. If databases are breached, individuals may suffer long-term consequences such as identity theft or surveillance without consent.

On the other hand, biometric authentication offers convenience and stronger security in many contexts. It reduces reliance on passwords, which are often weak, reused, or easily stolen. In banking, border control, and device security, biometrics can speed up verification and help prevent fraud.

However, even with these benefits, mandatory use raises ethical concerns, especially when participation is required for essential services. Therefore, consent must remain central, and alternatives like passwords or security tokens should always be available. Furthermore, governments and companies should ensure transparency about stored data and its usage.
